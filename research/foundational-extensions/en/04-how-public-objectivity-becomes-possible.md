# How Public Objectivity Becomes Possible — From Local Experience to Independent Testing

> Zhou Peng & Codex
> 2026-07-14
> A study of instruments, records, calibration, transformation closure, and non-arbitrary constraint

---

## Abstract

Objects, lengths, and clock readings first enter experience and operation from local positions. People need not have identical sensations, and instruments need not yield identical uncorrected readings. Public objectivity cannot mean eliminating every perspective, nor can it rest on internal coherence alone.

This study proposes: **public objectivity forms when local modes of access enter common constraint through publicly repeatable operations, traceable records, calibration relations, and transformations; those relations must converge through closed paths and independent tests while risking failure under new results.**

An instrument is not an observer without a subject, but a trace-structure others can re-enter. Calibration does not submit every device to an absolute standard; it establishes a traceable comparison network with uncertainty. Transformations let different expressions address one relational structure. Closure and independent paths prevent transformations from being merely internal stipulations.

Public objectivity is not a proof of final ontology. Games and formal systems may possess domain-internal objectivity. Physical objectivity additionally requires openness to measurements, interventions, and extrapolations not fully controlled by the present rules.

---

## I. What This Study Explains

The preceding studies supplied object reidentification, spatial measurement, and temporal measurement. This study adds:

- publicly re-enterable records;
- calibration and metrological traceability;
- transformations among perspectives and representations;
- closure tests for transformation loops;
- convergence of independent measurement paths;
- risk of extrapolative failure under new conditions.

It explains how public measurement is possible, not why any particular natural law has its particular form.

---

## II. Shared Experience Is Not Identical Experience

People at different positions do not receive identical images. Clocks following different paths need not accumulate identical readings. If objectivity required identical content from every perspective, real perspectival difference would defeat it.

A shared object instead requires that local conditions can be stated, reports can be transformed, transformed expectations can be checked by renewed operations, and discrepancies remain recorded rather than narratively erased.

> **Objectivity is not the absence of perspective but the presence of publicly testable relations among perspectives.**

---

## III. Records as Public Traces

Private memory is directly accessible only to its present subject. A public record can be re-entered by different subjects at different times under stated rules.

A record requires an event or reading, a carrier, a production procedure, rules of reading, metadata such as provenance, time, unit, and uncertainty, and a structure that makes untracked alteration detectable.

An instrument is therefore no magical source of pure objectivity. It organizes sensing, transduction, storage, and display into a reviewable operational and causal chain.

> **An instrument is a structure through which a local occurrence can be re-entered by others as a trace.**

---

## IV. Reproducibility and Accountable Difference

Repeating an experiment does not restore the whole world. It states which conditions must remain equivalent, which differences may be ignored, and which must be recorded.

Reproducibility requires transmissible procedures, public criteria for objects and variables, testable instrument response, stated uncertainty and exclusion rules, and retention of failures.

It does not demand numerically identical results. Differences should fall under an accountable model: random uncertainty, systematic bias, environmental influence, or theoretical failure.

---

## V. Calibration and Traceability

No ruler or clock guarantees its correctness under every condition. Calibration compares readings with reference realizations and records deviation, uncertainty, and scope.

A public metrological network contains unit definitions, multiple realizations, inter-instrument comparisons, calibration chains, uncertainty propagation, and cross-institutional review.

Traceability does not terminate in an absolute object outside relation. It links a result through a documented comparison chain to a shared reference and accounts for the uncertainty introduced along that chain.

---

## VI. Transformation Makes Perspectives Comparable

Coordinates, units, sensors, and report formats may yield different numbers. A transformation states how values change with representational position while preserving common operational consequences.

If A reports `x_A` and B reports `x_B`, a public transformation may state:

> `x_B = T_BA(x_A)`

Different numbers can then belong to one public relation. But a formula alone is insufficient. It must connect to instrument operations; its parameters must be estimable; its scope must be stated; and B must be able to check its transformed result independently.

---

## VII. Transformation Closure

With local systems A, B, and C, one result may be transformed along two paths:

> `A → B → C`
> `A → C`

The paths should converge within stated uncertainty. A loop `A → B → C → A` should return a value compatible with its start. An unexplained residue indicates that calibration, transformation, model, or stability assumptions require revision.

Closure is not formal consistency alone. It returns dispersed operations to measurable comparison and gives the network diagnostic power.

---

## VIII. Why Independent Paths Matter

Results from one sensor, one processing program, and one calibration source may agree because they share one bias.

Stronger public standing comes from paths as independent as practicable: instruments based on different principles, unit realizations in different laboratories, distinct signal routes, holdout data not used in fitting, and different analyses with compatible conclusions.

Independence is not absolute disconnection. It reduces the chance that one hidden local error jointly determines every result.

---

## IX. Why Coherence Is Not Enough

Fictional worlds, games, and formal systems may be coherent and support perspectives and coordinate transformations. They can possess **domain-internal objectivity**: within stated rules, judgments are not decided by one player.

That does not automatically make them knowledge of the physical world. Physical objectivity requires contact with new measurements not already absorbed into the construction.

We must distinguish domain-internal objectivity, empirical-physical objectivity constrained by independent data, intervention, and extrapolation, and the ontological assertion that the structure is the final form of the observer-independent world. The first two do not automatically prove the third.

---

## X. Extrapolation and Risk of Failure

A relation that only restates the data used to build it has not faced the unknown.

Physical standing requires extension to new times, places, instruments, experimenters, objects not used in calibration, changed controls, or greater precision and scale.

Failure must have consequences: narrowing scope, increasing uncertainty, revising transformations, replacing models, or abandoning an identification. If any result can be absorbed by changing every rule after the fact, the structure loses empirical content.

---

## XI. Convention and Phenomena

Unit names, coordinates, synchronization protocols, and formats contain conventions. Conventions select expressions but cannot alone determine all measurement consequences.

Conventions that transform explicitly while preserving operational predictions may be equivalent. If they differ in measurable expectations, the difference enters empirical testing.

> **Convention organizes comparison; phenomena restrict which organizations continue to work.**

Without convention, results cannot become public expressions. Without phenomenal constraint, conventions do not become physical knowledge.

---

## XII. Where Non-Arbitrariness Appears

Non-arbitrariness is not a thing hidden before measurement. It appears where established relations cannot determine what follows: an instrument violates a model, calibration chains fail to close, independent experiments disagree, extrapolation exceeds uncertainty, or intervention changes a claimed invariant.

We call the discontinuation failure and the difference forcing revision constraint or resistance.

> **Subjectivity is a relation established from a local position; public objectivity is its continuation not being decided by any one position alone.**

---

## XIII. What Follows and What Does Not

It follows that public objectivity does not require identical experience; instruments are re-enterable trace-structures; calibration is a relational network with uncertainty; transformations require closure and independent-path tests; and physical expressions must risk failure.

It does not follow that consensus guarantees truth, coherence proves physical reality, instruments are theory-free, calibration removes every bias, transformability uniquely determines nature, or public measurement proves final ontology.

---

## XIV. Answer

How does public objectivity become possible?

> **Local experience leaves re-enterable records through public operations; instruments and units are related by calibration chains with uncertainty; perspectives form common consequences through operational transformations; transformations face closed-loop and independent-path tests; and the structure is extrapolated to new conditions where it may fail. Relations thereby cease to be owned by any one subject, instrument, or theory.**

This is not a view from nowhere. It is a local network capable of reporting its conditions, errors, transformations, and failures.

The next study asks how such a public network compresses stable relations among variables into equations and sends them toward the unknown and the counterfactual as laws.
