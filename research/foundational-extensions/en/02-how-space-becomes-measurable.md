# How Space Becomes Measurable — From Maintained Difference to Invariant Relations

> Zhou Peng & Codex
> July 13, 2026
> A conceptual study of how space moves from difference into equality, units, distance, coordinates, and public measurement

---

## Abstract

*Before Consciousness* proposed that space is not an empty box that contains objects in advance, but the relation formed when multiple differences are simultaneously maintained.

Difference, however, can only establish "here and there" and "this and that." It cannot directly tell us how far apart two points are. Space arising does not mean that space is already measurable. To move from a space of differences into a metric space, a new structure is required: different spatial relations must be judged equal and made substitutable under specified transformations.

This study proposes: **space arises through difference, measurement through equality, and objectivity through relations that remain invariant under transformation.**

Measurement does not read numbers already written inside space. It selects stable boundaries, repeatedly covers a spatial difference with a unit maintained as equal, and records the number of repetitions. A unit is not one eternally unchanging object, but a class of relations substitutable under specified operations. A coordinate is not a label carried by the object itself, but a numerical expression of the object's relation to a public reference structure.

Multiple people do not need identical experiences in order to form a shared space. So long as different perspectives can be transformed into one another by stable rules, they can jointly point toward an objective relational structure. Physical objectivity therefore does not first mean eliminating observers. It means that among changing observational positions, instruments, and forms of expression, repeatable transformations and invariant relations can be found.

This study does not derive the dimensions, geometry, distance formula, or concrete laws of physical space from these structures. It investigates the conditions before such quantitative theories can begin: how difference becomes comparable, equality forms a unit, units form distance, distance enters coordinates, and different coordinates form public measurement.

---

## I. Beginning from a Space of Differences

Space first appears as difference.

"This" is not "that."

"Here" is not "there."

"Inside" is not "outside."

Only when these distinctions are simultaneously maintained can multiple objects, positions, and boundaries appear side by side.

Thus *Before Consciousness* compressed space into one sentence:

> **Space is the relation formed when differences are simultaneously maintained.**

But this sentence explains only how space can unfold into multiple positions. It does not explain how relations among positions can be assigned numbers.

Knowing that A differs from B is not knowing how far A is from B.

Knowing that a cup is on a table is not knowing how many centimeters it is from the table's edge.

Knowing that two people stand at different positions is not knowing their coordinates.

Difference makes space arise.

Measurement has not yet arisen.

---

## II. A Space of Differences and a Metric Space

Two levels must be distinguished.

The first is a space of differences:

- Objects differ from objects.
- There is inside and outside.
- There are adjacency, separation, and enclosure.
- There is here and there.

The second is a metric space:

- Distances between positions can be compared.
- Different intervals can be judged equal or unequal.
- A stable unit can be repeatedly arranged.
- Intervals can be expressed numerically.
- Different measurement results can be transformed into one another.

Moving from the first level into the second requires not more objects, but **comparison among relations**.

Space contains not only the three positions A, B, and C.

The two intervals A–B and B–C must also be compared.

Metric structure begins only when two intervals can be judged equal, greater, or smaller.

Therefore:

> **Spatial measurement is not the first division of objects and positions, but the relating-again of spatial relations themselves.**

---

## III. What Is Measured Is Not Space Itself

When measuring the length of a table, we do not place "space" itself on an instrument.

The actual operation is:

1. Point out one end of the table.
2. Point out the other end.
3. Select a unit of length.
4. Repeatedly cover the interval between the two ends with that unit.
5. Record how many times the unit repeats.

Measurement therefore directly encounters not space itself, but the relation between two boundaries.

Let the starting point be A, the endpoint B, and the unit relation U.

If repeating U n times reaches B from A, the distance is expressed as:

> `D(A,B) = nU`

The number n is not a property carried by space itself.

It comes from the number of repetitions of the unit relation.

Therefore:

> **Distance is the expression formed when a spatial difference is repeatedly counted with a unit maintained as equal.**

---

## IV. Equality Is Not Identity

The first centimeter and the second centimeter on a ruler are not the same thing.

They occupy different positions, involve different portions of material, and undergo different local changes.

If equality required all content to be completely identical, no two units could ever be equal.

Equality ignores differences irrelevant to the present measurement and preserves the relation capable of serving the same measuring role.

Thus:

> **Identity is different manifestations attributed to the same object.**
>
> **Equality is different objects or relations attributed to the same substitutable class.**

The first and second centimeters are not the same piece of material, yet they can substitute for each other in length measurement.

A metal ruler and a wooden ruler are not the same object, yet after calibration they can express the same length.

Equality is not the discovery of an identical essence hidden inside two things.

Equality establishes that, under specified operations, replacing one with the other does not change the relevant result.

Therefore:

> **Equality is constrained substitutability.**

---

## V. How Equality Is Operationally Produced

Suppose there are two intervals: A–B and C–D.

Move the first interval to the position of the second:

- A coincides with C.
- B also coincides with D.

We then say that the two intervals have equal length.

No abstract "length itself" has been directly seen.

What occurs is a transformation: after a change of position, the first relation can replace the second while preserving the coincidence of both boundaries.

This can be expressed as:

> If there is a transformation `T` such that `T(A)=C` and `T(B)=D`, and the measured relation is preserved through the transformation, then A–B and C–D are equal in length.

Thus:

> **Equal length means that two spatial relations can coincide under specified transformations.**

This turns "equality" from an abstract judgment into a repeatable operation.

Different people need not directly share the same sensation. So long as they can perform the same operation and obtain the same coincidence relation within specified error, they can jointly maintain that the two intervals are equal.

---

## VI. Allowed Transformations and Invariant Relations

Not every change can be used to judge equal length.

A ruler moved from the left side of a table to the right is usually treated as retaining the same length.

Turning the ruler usually does not change the length it expresses either.

But after stretching, compressing, or heating the ruler, one cannot continue using the old unit without correction.

Measurement must therefore distinguish:

- Transformations that do not change the measured relation.
- Transformations that do change the measured relation and require recording or correction.

Equality always holds relative to a set of allowed transformations.

Relations unchanged under those transformations become the stable core of measurement.

Thus:

> **Equality is the relation that remains after difference passes through allowed transformation.**

Position can change.

Direction can change.

The observer can change.

The form of expression can change.

So long as the relevant relation remains invariant, or changes according to a stable rule, measurement can continue across those changes.

---

## VII. A Unit Is Not a Thing

A meter is not one wooden bar that remains unchanged forever.

Concrete rulers deform, deteriorate, and disappear.

A meter is the equality relation maintained among many concrete realizations: implementations in different rulers, positions, and times can, after specified calibration, serve the same measuring role.

Therefore:

> **A unit is not a single object; it is a class of relations substitutable under specified operations.**

Concrete rulers, marks, and instruments are carriers of the unit.

The unit itself is a correspondence maintained across carriers.

This means that the unit does not depend on one object remaining eternally unchanged. It depends on:

- Reproducible production.
- Reproducible calibration.
- Identifiable environmental effects.
- Comparability among different realizations.
- Long-term mutual agreement among comparison results.

The stability of a unit is not the absolute changelessness of an object.

It is a network of relations continually re-establishing equality.

---

## VIII. Repetition, Composition, and Number

Once a unit is established, it can be repeated.

One unit.

Two units.

Three units.

Multiple unit relations judged equal are composed in sequence to form a larger spatial relation.

Number records not the content of space, but how many unit relations must be composed to move from the starting point to the endpoint.

The derivation is therefore not "space naturally contains numbers," but:

> Equality establishes the unit.
>
> The unit permits repetition.
>
> Repetition permits counting.
>
> Counting forms numerical value.
>
> Numerical value expresses distance.

Measurement converts continuous spatial difference into a discrete number of unit repetitions.

If the endpoint does not fall exactly on a whole unit, the unit is subdivided further, or the remaining relation is expressed as a ratio.

Numerical precision therefore comes from the continued divisibility and comparability of unit relations, not from decimals hidden somewhere inside space.

---

## IX. Coordinates Are Public Reference Relations

Distance expresses only how far apart two positions are.

To express where an object is, a reference structure is also required:

- Select an origin.
- Select directions.
- Select a unit.
- Repeat the unit along those directions.
- Mark positions with numbers.

Coordinates then appear.

A coordinate is not a label carried by the object itself.

It is the numerical expression of the object's relation to a reference structure.

The same object can have different coordinates in different coordinate systems.

This does not mean that only one coordinate is correct and all others are wrong.

So long as stable transformations exist among different coordinates, they can express the same relational structure.

Therefore:

> **Objectivity does not require every observer to give the same coordinate; it requires different coordinates to be transformable into one another by stable rules.**

---

## X. Shared Space Is Not Identical Experience

One person observes a rock from the left, another from the right.

Their color distributions, outlines, directions, and bodily sensations differ.

If objectivity required two people to have completely identical experiences, shared space could never arise.

But when the first person moves to the second person's position, they can obtain an appearance similar to what the second person had before. When the second moves to the first person's position, the reverse can occur.

The contents of their experiences differ, yet the transformation between the experiences can be stably repeated.

The shared object is therefore not a perfectly identical image inside two minds, nor need it be imagined as a third image hiding behind all experience.

The shared object can be understood as:

> **The transformation structure continually maintained among multiple different experiences.**

Thus:

> **The objective is not the absence of perspective; the objective is stable transformation among perspectives.**

Different observers do not need to erase their own positions.

They need to be able to state how appearances and measurements change when positions change.

---

## XI. How Instruments Form Public Traces

One person cannot hand the exact content of their visual manifestation to another.

An instrument changes this, not because it has no perspective, but because it can leave a trace that multiple perspectives can repeatedly read.

A ruler leaves relations of coincident marks.

A camera leaves an image record.

A sensor converts interaction into numbers.

One person can read the record, another can read it again, and another instrument can process the same record.

An instrument is therefore not an absolutely objective observer.

It is:

> **A structure that turns one interaction into a stable trace that different observers can re-enter at different times.**

For one spatial measurement to become public measurement, at least the following are needed:

1. Object boundaries can be stably indicated.
2. The operational procedure can be repeated.
3. Instrument states can leave records.
4. The form of record can be read by multiple people.
5. Different readings can be compared by rules.

Publicity is not the disappearance of individual experience.

Publicity is individual experiences entering into relation through the same trace.

---

## XII. Mutual Calibration, Not an Absolute Ruler

How do we know that a ruler has not changed length after being moved?

It can be compared with another ruler.

How do we know that the second ruler is stable?

Further standards, production procedures, and measurement results are required.

In the end, there is no ruler standing outside all measurement and providing an absolute guarantee for every equality.

What actually forms is a network of mutual calibration:

- Multiple rulers are compared.
- Different materials are compared.
- Production procedures are repeatedly tested.
- Environmental changes are recorded and corrected.
- Different observers repeat measurements.
- Measurement results are used to predict later phenomena.
- New phenomena test the existing units and rules again.

When these relations continue to agree over time, certain units, operations, and transformations are maintained as stable.

Therefore:

> **The objectivity of measurement comes not from an absolutely unchanging object, but from continuing agreement among relations through replication, transformation, and comparison.**

---

## XIII. The Circle of Measurement

Measuring space requires an equal unit.

Confirming that units are equal requires comparison of their spatial relations.

This forms a loop:

- Equality establishes measurement.
- Measurement tests equality.

If an absolute starting point entirely independent of measurement is demanded, the loop cannot end.

Actual measurement, however, does not begin from one unconditional first unit.

It gradually stabilizes through mutual constraint within a relational network: whether a unit holds depends on whether it can remain consistent with other units, instruments, operations, and predictions.

The circle here is therefore not merely an error. It is the form of the measurement structure.

Units support measurement.

Measurement in turn maintains and corrects units.

Reference systems support coordinates.

Coordinate transformations in turn test relations among reference systems.

No single element independently grounds all objectivity.

**Objectivity is the entire relational network remaining coherent through repeated collision.**

---

## XIV. From Invariant Relations to Physical Laws

One measurement gives one result.

When position, direction, observer, instrument, and form of expression change, repeated measurements reveal two kinds of structure.

The first consists of relations that remain unchanged under allowed transformations.

The second consists of relations that change, but always according to the same rule.

The former become invariants.

The latter become transformation laws.

Together, they allow results under different observational conditions to be organized into one description.

Therefore:

> **Physical objectivity is not the absence of change, but the presence of stable invariants and transformation rules within change.**

Physical laws can be understood as compressed expressions of these stable relations across measurements.

But this study goes only as far as that threshold.

It does not derive a concrete distance formula, geometric structure, or physical law directly from concepts. Which relations remain invariant in actual measurement must be determined jointly by operations, instruments, and phenomena.

---

## XV. Boundaries of the Study

This study has not demonstrated that:

- Space necessarily has a particular number of dimensions.
- Space necessarily obeys a particular geometry.
- All observers necessarily share one distance.
- Any concrete ruler retains its length under all conditions.
- Experiential space can directly replace physical spacetime.
- Concrete physical laws can be derived from conscious maintenance alone.

It proposes the conceptual conditions under which measurement can arise:

- Difference can be stably indicated.
- Relations can be compared.
- Equality can be operationally tested.
- Units can be replicated and composed.
- Results can be recorded.
- Different perspectives can be transformed.
- Stable relations exist through transformation.

Which units, geometries, and transformation laws the actual world uses cannot be determined by conceptual analysis alone.

---

## XVI. The Core Model

This study can be compressed into:

> **Difference maintained — space.**
>
> **Relations made comparable — equality.**
>
> **An equal relation selected as a standard — unit.**
>
> **Units repeatedly composed — measurement.**
>
> **Repetitions recorded — distance.**
>
> **Distance entered into a public reference structure — coordinates.**
>
> **Different coordinates stably transformed — shared space.**
>
> **Relations preserved or changing by rule under transformation — physical objectivity.**

This is not a one-way process deriving number from a substance called space.

Each level maintains the level before it in return: measurement calibrates units, coordinate transformations test distance relations, and new observations correct the allowed transformations.

The whole structure stabilizes through repeated comparison.

---

## XVII. How Space Becomes Measurable

Now return to the question in the title.

Why can space be measured?

Not because space naturally contains numbers.

Not because an absolutely unchanging ruler stands outside all relations.

Nor because all observers see exactly the same space.

Space can be measured because:

> **Spatial differences can be organized into relations that are comparable, substitutable, repeatable, composable, recordable, and transformable across perspectives.**

Yet saying "space can be measured" can again make measurability appear to be an eternal property of space itself.

Strictly speaking, space does not perform measurement, and numbers are not hidden in space waiting to be read.

Only when objects, boundaries, units, instruments, observers, records, and transformations jointly form a measurement structure do spatial differences appear as distances and coordinates.

The following must therefore be taken back:

> Space is not first completed as an object and then given "measurability" as an added property.

More precisely:

> **When difference enters stable comparison, space arises in a measurable form.**

"Space" and "the measurement of space" are not two completely separate layers.

Measurement does not stand outside space and describe it.

Measurement itself is one way spatial relation continues to unfold.
