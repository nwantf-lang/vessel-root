# How Time Becomes Measurable — From Repeated Change to Public Clocks

> Zhou Peng & Codex
> July 13, 2026
> A conceptual study of how time moves from events, repetition, and traces into clock readings, remote synchronization, and public time

---

## Abstract

Spatial units can coexist side by side: one centimeter can be placed beside another, forming distance through coincidence, substitution, and repetition. Temporal units cannot coexist. The first oscillation has already disappeared when the second occurs. Two temporal intervals cannot be moved into the same moment and made to coincide directly.

Temporal measurement therefore faces a problem different from spatial measurement: **why can two intervals that cannot coexist be judged equal?**

This study proposes that temporal measurement does not use something unchanging to read an independently flowing time. It uses one change, selected as a standard, to compare another change. Spatial units acquire equality through juxtaposition; temporal units acquire equality through recurrence. A repeating process provides the unit, an accumulating trace preserves the count, and the two together form a clock.

Recurrence does not mean that the entire world returns to its previous state. A cycle arises when a selected set of relevant states again satisfies the same criterion. A temporal unit is not a preserved piece of time, but a class of state-transition relations that can be repeatedly realized and substituted for one another within specified precision.

One clock can form only a local reading along its own path. Shared time among distant clocks additionally requires signal exchange, synchronization protocols, calibration networks, and stable transformation rules. Public time is not a single number already flowing behind all locations, but a repeatable relational structure formed among the records of multiple local clocks.

This study does not derive the concrete equations of relativity from conceptual analysis, nor does it equate synchronization conventions with arbitrary invention. It investigates the structural conditions under which physical time measurement can begin: how events form intervals, repetition forms units, traces form counts, signals connect distant readings, and public time arises from networks of local clocks.

---

## I. Where Temporal and Spatial Measurement Diverge

In spatial measurement, one unit can be placed beside another.

Two lengths can be moved to the same position so that their starting points and endpoints coincide.

Equality arises through relations of juxtaposition, coincidence, and substitution.

Temporal measurement is different.

When the first second occurs, the second has not yet occurred.

When the second occurs, the first has already passed.

Two durations cannot be placed side by side at once, and a past second cannot be carried into the present to coincide directly with the current second.

Therefore:

> **Spatial units acquire equality through juxtaposed substitution; temporal units acquire equality through recurring realization.**

This is not merely a difference in instrument technique. It is a difference in measurement structure.

Spatial measurement primarily handles differences that coexist.

Temporal measurement must handle how a change that has disappeared can enter into relation with a present change.

---

## II. A Temporal Interval Is Not an Independent Thing

Measuring a duration first requires two boundary events.

Event A: the process begins.

Event B: the process ends.

A and B by themselves are simply two occurrences. Only when they are connected as the starting point and endpoint of the same process does "the interval from A to B" arise.

Pressing a button and a light turning on can be connected as one response process.

Sunrise and sunset can be connected as a span of change within a day.

Sending a signal and receiving its echo can be connected as one round trip.

Therefore:

> **A temporal interval is two events maintained as the starting point and endpoint of the same process.**

An interval is not a substance floating between events.

It is a relation of continuity established between them.

If two events are not attributed to the same process, no automatically existing "time in between" waits to be measured.

---

## III. Comparing Change with Change

A clock does not touch an independently flowing time.

The clock itself changes:

- A pendulum moves back and forth.
- A crystal oscillates.
- An atomic system produces a specified frequency.
- A circuit counts cycles.
- A display leaves a reading.

When we say that a process lasts ten seconds, what we actually express is that, between its starting and ending events, the change selected as a standard completed a specified number of repetitions.

Therefore:

> **Temporal measurement does not use the unchanging to measure change; it uses standard change to compare other change.**

The measured process and the clock process unfold together.

The change of one process is expressed by the repetition count of another.

A time value is not a label carried inside an event. It is a relation formed after two forms of change are compared.

---

## IV. Recurrence Is Not the Restoration of the World

After the first cycle ends, the world has changed.

If the entire world truly returned to exactly the same state:

- The counter would return to its previous number.
- The instrument would retain no first cycle.
- The observer would not remember that it happened.
- No trace could distinguish a first cycle from a second.

Complete restoration would erase all evidence that repetition had occurred.

The recurrence required by a clock is therefore not identity of all content.

It is a selected relation again satisfying the same condition.

A pendulum returns to a specified position and direction of motion.

An oscillator completes one phase cycle.

A detector produces the specified pulse again.

Therefore:

> **Recurrence is not the world returning to the same state, but relevant states re-entering the same criterion-class.**

---

## V. A Cycle Is a Rule for Judging State

Returning to the same position alone may not constitute a complete cycle.

A pendulum can pass its lowest point twice in one round trip, but in opposite directions.

A cycle boundary therefore requires a fuller set of criteria, such as:

- Reaching a specified position.
- Having a specified direction of motion.
- Satisfying a specified phase.
- Completing a specified state-transition path.

Each time these conditions are satisfied again, the instrument produces one tick.

The first tick and the second tick are not the same event.

They belong to the same class because both trigger the same rules of recognition and response.

Thus:

> **A cycle is not the same event appearing again, but different events attributed to the same class of state transition.**

A clock need not read the entire state of the world.

It is sensitive only to differences relevant to its cycle criterion and ignores the others.

---

## VI. How Temporal Units Acquire Equality

Two temporal cycles cannot coincide simultaneously.

Their equality comes from substitutability: when comparing other changes, replacing one cycle with another does not alter the result within specified precision.

If a process lasts ten cycles, and the count and prediction remain consistent whichever stable set of cycles from the same class is used, those cycles are maintained as equivalent.

Therefore:

> **A temporal unit is a class of state-transition relations substitutable under specified operations and precision.**

This corresponds to the spatial unit:

- Spatial units maintain equality across positions.
- Temporal units maintain equality across before and after.

Spatial units are tested through movement and coincidence.

Temporal units are tested through recurrence, counting, and long-term comparison with other cycles.

Equality is not an unchanging entity inside a cycle.

It is continuing agreement among multiple relations of change through repeated comparison.

---

## VII. A Clock Requires Both Cycle and Accumulation

A cycle provides only a repeatable unit.

If the instrument completely returns to its original state every cycle, it cannot tell us how many cycles have passed.

A complete clock requires two different structures:

1. **Cyclic structure**
   - Returns to the same criterion-boundary.
   - Produces a repeatable unit.
   - Maintains "this occurrence is the same kind as the previous one."

2. **Accumulating structure**
   - Changes state whenever a cycle completes.
   - Preserves the number already completed.
   - Maintains "this is the second occurrence, not the first."

The pendulum can oscillate.

The counter must change from 0 to 1, 2, 3, and onward.

Therefore:

> **Cycle provides scale; accumulation provides elapsed count.**

Without cycle, time has no stable unit.

Without accumulation, the cycle has only phase and no indication of how much has elapsed.

---

## VIII. Traces Establish Before and After

The first tick has disappeared.

When the second tick occurs, the first remains only as counter state, memory, or record.

The present reading 2 contains an asymmetric structure:

- The first occurrence has already left a trace.
- The second has just been counted.
- The third has not yet occurred.

"The second is after the first" is not a label carried inside the second event.

It comes from the present structure already containing a trace of the first.

Thus:

> **The past is not an event that remains present, but a difference preserved within the current structure.**

Periodic repetition alone need not provide direction.

Looking only at the periodic trajectory of an ideal pendulum, it may be difficult to distinguish a recording played forward from one played backward.

Temporal direction comes from asymmetric accumulation of traces: records increase, memory forms, and the count is not reset each cycle.

Therefore:

> **Repetition makes time countable; traces give time an order of before and after.**

---

## IX. Exact Equality, Uncertainty, and Calibration

The period of a concrete clock is affected by its environment and implementation:

- Temperature change.
- Friction and energy loss.
- Electromagnetic disturbance.
- Manufacturing variation.
- Detection noise.
- Aging of the apparatus.

Two physically realized cycles are therefore unlikely to be absolutely identical.

"Equality" in measurement always holds relative to specified conditions, procedures, and uncertainty.

We must distinguish:

- The unit in a model: a relation defined as exactly equal.
- The realization in a clock: an approximation to that relation within finite uncertainty.

Whether a periodic process can become a public standard is not decided by an individual.

It must be:

- Stably identifiable.
- Reproducible in different devices.
- Comparable with other clocks over time.
- Able to account for and correct environmental effects.
- Able to support stable predictions of other phenomena.

Therefore:

> **A time standard is not an arbitrarily declared repetition, but a recurring relation that remains stable through comparison across devices.**

---

## X. The Second: Definition and Realization

Modern metrology does not tie "one second" to one eternally unchanging clock.

The International System of Units defines the second by fixing the numerical value of the unperturbed ground-state hyperfine transition frequency of the caesium-133 atom at 9,192,631,770 when expressed in Hz, equal to s⁻¹. The effect is that one second corresponds to 9,192,631,770 periods of that radiation.

The structure developed in this study remains visible:

- Select a state transition capable of highly stable realization.
- Define a specified number of periods as one unit.
- Realize the definition with concrete apparatus.
- State uncertainty for the practical realization.
- Compare realizations among different laboratories.

The definition is not the concrete reading of one atomic clock.

A concrete atomic clock is one finite-precision realization of the definition.

Therefore:

> **The temporal unit remains exact as a relational definition; a physical clock remains conditional and uncertain as a concrete realization.**

This distinction prevents "the unit" from being mistaken for one absolutely unchanging object.

---

## XI. One Clock Produces Only Local Time

The cycles, counts, and records of a clock all occur along the path it experiences.

It can stably answer:

> From local event A to local event B, how many cycles did this clock accumulate?

Its own reading alone cannot directly answer what a distant clock shows "now."

Therefore:

> **A local clock produces a local reading; public time requires relations among different local readings.**

Two clocks do not become synchronized merely by using the same unit.

One must also know:

- When they began counting.
- Whether they were calibrated at the same location.
- What paths and environments they experienced after separation.
- How readings are transmitted between locations.
- How signal propagation is handled.

A shared unit makes readings comparable.

Synchronization additionally establishes a common origin and remote correspondence among readings.

---

## XII. Distant Readings Must Enter Through Signals

Clock A asks a distant clock B, "What time is it now?"

B returns its reading.

By the time A receives the answer, the event in which B sent it has already passed.

What A directly receives is not B's present state, but a trace left at an earlier event and transmitted from B.

Inferring a distant reading requires handling signal-propagation time.

But expressing one-way propagation time seems already to require synchronized clocks at both locations.

A loop appears:

- Distant synchronization requires knowledge of signal-propagation relations.
- Expressing one-way propagation time requires synchronized distant clocks.

This shows that distant simultaneity cannot be read directly like local coincidence.

It requires signal exchange and an explicit rule of organization.

---

## XIII. Synchronization Protocols and Distant Simultaneity

Let A send a signal at reading `t₁`.

B receives and returns the signal.

A receives the echo at reading `t₃`.

A can directly record the total round-trip interval `t₃ - t₁`.

If a synchronization rule assuming symmetric outbound and return propagation conditions is adopted, the event of B receiving the signal is assigned:

> `t₂ = (t₁ + t₃) / 2`

This rule divides the round-trip interval in half and establishes a remote clock relation between A and B.

The key point is:

> **Distant simultaneity is not one direct perception, but a relation jointly established by signal exchange, propagation assumptions, and synchronization protocol.**

The protocol contains convention, but is not arbitrary fantasy.

It must extend to multiple clocks, remain coherent under repeated exchanges, and keep different paths, predictions, and instrument records in continuing agreement.

Convention selects the form of expression.

Phenomena test whether that expression can work stably.

---

## XIV. Public Time Is a Network of Clocks

After two clocks are synchronized, a third, fourth, and many more devices can be added.

Different locations exchange timing signals.

Transmission results along different paths are compared.

Clocks report differences in frequency and reading.

The system corrects for known effects and forms a coordinated time scale.

Coordinated Universal Time is not the reading directly given by one global master clock. National metrology institutes and observatories maintain local realizations and submit clock data to the BIPM, where the data contribute to a coordinated time scale.

A public time system therefore contains at least:

1. Local periodic standards.
2. Accumulated cycle readings.
3. Exchange of timing signals.
4. Remote synchronization protocols.
5. Cross-clock calibration.
6. Corrections for known effects.
7. Public records readable by people and instruments.

Therefore:

> **Public time is not something owned by a single clock, but a relational network formed among local clocks through signals, calibration, and rules.**

## XV. How a Shared “Now” Appears

Only local events occur directly at each position.

A distant "now" cannot be immediately experienced by one observer.

Only when a synchronization protocol places clock readings at different locations into one time coordinate does the following arise:

> This event at A and that event at B are simultaneous.

We must therefore distinguish:

- **Local now**: the event occurring along a local path.
- **Public now**: a set of distant events assigned the same time value by synchronization and coordinate rules.

The public now is constructed, but construction does not mean falsehood.

Coordinates are also constructed, yet they support measurement and prediction through stable transformation.

More precisely:

> **Distant simultaneity is a relational rule organizing separated events within one temporal expression.**

Coordinate systems in different states of motion may divide distant events into simultaneous sets differently.

Objectivity need not require every observer to share one distant "now."

It requires different divisions to be stably transformable and consistent with signals and clock records.

---

## XVI. Different Clock Readings Are Not Necessarily Errors

Two clocks can be set to the same reading at one location, follow different paths, and show different accumulated values when they meet again.

If the difference is repeatable, predictable, and consistent with other instruments and phenomena, one clock cannot simply be declared defective.

Each clock records accumulated time along its own path.

Modern metrology explicitly understands the SI second as a unit of proper time in the relativistic sense; forming coordinated time scales from clocks at different locations requires corrections for relativistic frequency shifts.

This means:

> **Objective time does not require every good clock on every path always to show the same number.**

Equal readings form one stable relation.

Predictably different readings can form another stable relation.

Temporal objectivity lies not only in clocks being the same, but in why they differ being explainable by shared rules.

---

## XVII. Temporal Objectivity Is Transformability

Traditional intuition treats public time as one unified number already flowing through the universe, with all ideal clocks merely reading it.

This study moves toward another expression:

- Each clock produces local cycles and readings.
- Clocks enter into relation through meetings or signals.
- Synchronization protocols form remote time coordinates.
- Different coordinates may give different readings and divisions of simultaneity.
- Transformation rules explain how those differences correspond.
- Some event relations remain stable through transformation.

Therefore:

> **Temporal objectivity does not consist in every clock possessing the same number, but in stable, repeatable, testable transformations among local readings.**

This connects to the study of space:

Objective space does not require all observers to give the same coordinates.

Objective time does not require all observers to give the same "now."

Both require measurement relations to transform by rules after perspective changes.

---

## XVIII. The Circle of Temporal Measurement

Defining a temporal unit requires equal cycles.

Judging cycles equal seems to require an already equal time.

Synchronizing distant clocks requires signal-propagation relations.

Expressing one-way propagation time requires synchronized clocks.

Temporal measurement therefore contains multiple loops.

If an absolute starting point independent of every clock, signal, and protocol is demanded, these loops cannot terminate.

Actual metrology stabilizes through a relational network:

- Different kinds of clocks are compared over time.
- Concrete realizations are made traceable to the unit definition.
- Signal paths are repeatedly calibrated.
- Environmental and motional effects are modeled and corrected.
- Readings are used to predict other phenomena.
- New comparisons continue testing existing standards.

The loop therefore cannot be closed simply by saying "time is defined by clocks."

More precisely:

> **Units, clocks, signals, protocols, and phenomena mutually constrain one another and jointly maintain measurable time.**

No single element stands outside the network and grounds all time alone.

## XIX. Boundaries of the Study

This study has not demonstrated that:

- Time is only a subjective illusion.
- Physical change can be completely described without time.
- Any repeating process can serve as an equally good clock.
- Synchronization conventions can ignore signal propagation and actual phenomena.
- All observers necessarily share one "now."
- The concrete equations of relativity can be derived directly from "maintenance."
- Accumulated traces alone explain every problem concerning the direction of time.

It proposes the conceptual structure under which temporal measurement can arise:

- Events form boundaries.
- Boundaries are connected into intervals.
- State transitions can recur.
- Recurrences are attributed to an equivalence class.
- Cycles trigger counts.
- Counts leave accumulating traces.
- Signals connect distant records.
- Synchronization protocols organize public coordinates.
- Transformation rules relate different local times.

Which processes are most stable, which corrections are required, and which relations remain invariant in reality must be determined jointly by physical theory and experimental measurement.

## XXI. How Time Becomes Measurable

Now return to the question in the title.

Why can time be measured?

Not because a clock touches an independently flowing temporal substance.

Not because two vanished intervals can be juxtaposed again.

Nor because one number exists throughout the universe that every clock must read.

Time can be measured because:

> **Events can form intervals, state transitions can recur, recurrences can be grouped into equivalent units, counts can leave accumulating traces, and local records can form public relations through signals, synchronization, and transformation.**

Yet saying "time can be measured" can again establish time as a completed object and then assign measurability to it.

Strictly speaking, time does not first flow and then get read by clocks.

Only when events, cycles, counts, records, signals, and synchronization jointly form a measurement structure does change appear as clock readings and temporal coordinates.

The following must therefore be taken back:

> Time is not first completed as a thing and then given numbers by clocks.

More precisely:

> **When change enters stable recurrence, accumulated record, and cross-location transformation, time arises in a measurable form.**

A clock does not stand outside time and describe it.

The clock itself is one structure through which temporal relations continue to unfold.
