# How Laws Become Possible — From Public Measurement to Fallible Prediction

> Zhou Peng & Codex
> July 14, 2026
> A conceptual study of how physical laws arise through measurement, transformation, invariants, prediction, and public testing

---

## Abstract

The first four studies established object reidentification, spatial organization and metric, temporal organization and measurement, and public objectivity through calibration, closure, and independent testing. This study asks how public measurement becomes a physical law capable of prediction across conditions and defeat by new results.

This study proposes: **a physical law is not an unchanging thing or a commander hidden behind phenomena; it is a relational structure capable of maintaining consistent, testable predictions across descriptions, experiments, and counterfactual conditions.**

Concrete values can change with coordinates, units, and observational position. Objectivity does not require every observer to produce the same numbers. It requires stable transformations among different expressions. Some relations remain unchanged under transformation, while others change together according to definite rules. The former become invariants; the latter form covariant structures.

Laws are not automatically produced by finite data. Variable selection, experimental equivalence, mathematical form, domain, and synchronization rules all contain construction. Yet whether a new experiment agrees with a prediction is not under arbitrary personal control. The expression of a law belongs to observation; its constraint comes from collision with phenomena; its objectivity comes from that constraint not being arbitrarily rewritable.

This study distinguishes the expression of a law from the stable constraint toward which the law points. Without observation, records, and theoretical language, no law exists as a stated law. But this does not mean that personal consciousness can freely create or cancel stable relations. When observation occurs again, present traces, instrument results, and different observers jointly constrain which relations can continue to be maintained as laws.

This study does not claim to solve the problem of induction, nor does it treat symmetry, mathematical simplicity, or predictive success as proof of final truth. It examines the structural conditions under which laws become public physical knowledge, and how those conditions finally withdraw the position of "law" as an independent existent.

---

## I. The Common Question Left by the First Four Studies

The first study showed that objecthood depends on conditional reidentification and that objectual existence cannot replace existence in general. The second and third openly added conditions for spatial and temporal organization, measurement, and synchronization. The fourth further required transformation closure, independent-path convergence, and extrapolative risk.

A fifth question follows:

> When coordinates, units, clocks, paths, instruments, and observers can all change, what is maintained as the same law?

A law clearly cannot be one fixed numerical value.

Different observers can record different coordinates.

Different units produce different numbers.

Different local clocks can accumulate different readings.

If a law required all surface data to be identical, it could not cross any perspective.

The fifth study therefore begins from how public relations enter the unknown.

---

## II. Variables Are Selections from Manifestation

One physical occurrence contains many differences:

- Position.
- Velocity.
- Temperature.
- Material state.
- Electromagnetic environment.
- Instrument noise.
- Observer position.
- Form of record.

No experiment puts every detail into one equation at once.

Researchers select certain differences as variables and temporarily ignore others.

Variables are not labels already attached to phenomena themselves.

They are parts drawn from the whole occurrence so that they can be repeatedly recognized and compared.

Therefore:

> **A variable is a name given to manifestation after it has been organized into measurable difference.**

Variable selection determines what is seen and what temporarily remains outside the law.

A law does not directly face "the entire world." It faces a research structure jointly drawn by object boundaries, measurement procedures, and variables.

---

## III. Records Are Not Laws

One measurement gives one value.

Multiple measurements give a set of records.

Records tell only what occurred under certain conditions.

For example:

| Time | Position |
|---:|---:|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |

Writing these records as `x = 2t` does what the records alone did not:

- Compresses multiple results into one relation.
- Connects measured positions.
- Gives predictions for unmeasured positions.
- Excludes some possible results.
- Allows transformation among units and coordinates.

Therefore:

> **Data state what occurred; a law proposes how quantities must relate under different conditions.**

An isolated record contains no law.

A law begins to appear in relations among records that remain comparable.

---

## IV. Repeated Experiment Is Not the World Repeated Identically

A second experiment cannot match the first in every detail.

The time is different.

The instrument's internal state is different.

The environment contains small differences.

The operator and recording process may also differ.

A repeated experiment means that some relevant conditions are maintained as equivalent while other differences are allowed to vary.

It must therefore specify:

- Which conditions must remain.
- Which differences can be ignored.
- How much error is allowed.
- What counts as agreement.
- What constitutes failure.

Thus:

> **A repeated experiment is not the same event happening again, but different events again satisfying the same experimental criterion.**

This has the same structure as cycles and clocks.

What is maintained is not identical content, but relevant relations continuing to fall within the same comparable class.

---

## V. Descriptive and Physical Transformations

The same object can receive different position values in different coordinate systems.

The same length can be written as 1 meter or 100 centimeters.

The same temperature can be expressed on different scales.

These changes first alter the description and need not alter the physical configuration described.

They can be called descriptive transformations.

Actually moving an object, heating a system, changing voltage, or replacing material changes relations between object and environment.

These can be called physical transformations.

The initial distinction is:

> **A descriptive transformation changes expression; a physical transformation changes experimental configuration.**

But the boundary cannot be determined by numerical change alone.

If an entire closed experiment, including instruments, observers, and references, is moved together, its internal relations may remain unchanged.

A physical change must ultimately specify what was transformed, what was not, and which relations changed as a result.

---

## VI. Passive and Active Transformations

A passive transformation does not move the object. It redescribes the same physical situation.

Examples include changing coordinate origins, axis directions, units, or labels.

An active transformation changes the object or experimental arrangement and then measures again.

Examples include rotating the apparatus, moving the object, or changing an external condition.

The two test different questions:

> **Passive transformation tests whether different descriptions maintain the same physical content.**
>
> **Active transformation tests whether the lawful relation remains after the physical configuration changes.**

If an actively transformed experiment produces the same relational structure under corresponding conditions, the transformation exhibits a symmetry.

Symmetry does not mean that nothing happened.

Position, direction, or arrangement may have changed.

It means that relevant measurements cannot distinguish the lawful structure before and after transformation.

---

## VII. One Physical Situation Is an Equivalence Class of Descriptions

Two descriptions can use different numbers, symbols, units, and coordinates.

If they give the same results or stably transformable results for every relevant measurement, they are attributed to the same physical situation.

Therefore:

> **One physical situation does not mean identical descriptive content, but identical or transformable measurable consequences.**

This continues the distinction among identity, equality, and public transformation developed in the first four studies.

One object is not invariant content, but continuing correspondence among differing manifestations.

Likewise, one physical situation is not a fixed image hiding behind descriptions, but a measurable equivalence relation maintained among them.

Descriptive equivalence is not declared by language alone.

Research continually searches for distinguishable consequences. If new measurement distinguishes two descriptions, the former equivalence class must be divided.

---

## VIII. Invariants, Covariance, and Symmetry

Stability under transformation has at least three forms.

The first is numerical invariance: a quantity retains the same value before and after transformation.

The second is relational invariance: individual values change, but a combination or event relation remains.

The third is preservation of form: variables transform together by rules while the equation's form and testable predictions remain consistent.

The first two approach invariants.

The third expresses covariant structure.

We therefore cannot ask only:

> Which number did not change?

We must also ask:

> Which relation remained? How did variables change together? Do different expressions give the same measurement predictions?

Thus:

> **Objectivity comes not only from remaining unchanged, but from changing according to stable rules.**

Symmetry groups many apparently different configurations into one lawful class.

Covariance lets different descriptions continue to correspond through change.

---

## IX. Exact and Approximate Symmetry

A transformation can preserve a law exactly within a theoretical model.

Real apparatus always contains environmental differences, manufacturing error, and finite precision.

We must therefore distinguish:

- Exact symmetry: holds strictly within a given theoretical structure.
- Approximate symmetry: holds within a specified scale, condition, or precision.

An apparatus may appear rotationally symmetric at low precision while higher precision reveals deviations from external fields or material differences.

Every symmetry claim must therefore state:

- What system it applies to.
- Which external relations are ignored.
- What error is allowed.
- At what scale it applies.
- What result would break it.

"Everything is the same" without a domain is not a testable physical symmetry.

---

## X. Equations Compress Joint Change

An equation does not require all variables to retain the same values.

It expresses how other quantities must change together when one quantity changes.

An equation therefore maintains not numerical identity, but relational form.

For example, `x = 2t` does more than summarize four known points.

It also:

- Derives `x` for other values of `t`.
- Excludes results that fail the relation under the same conditions.
- Lets variables correspond through unit and coordinate changes.
- Organizes different experiments within one calculational structure.

Thus:

> **An equation is a compressed expression of how differences change together.**

Compression does not retain every detail.

It selects relations considered relevant and ignores differences that do not presently affect prediction.

---

## XI. An Equation Is Not a Physical Law by Itself

Any equation can exist as a mathematical object.

To become a physical law, it must also state:

- How variables are measured.
- What units are used.
- How instruments operate.
- What the initial conditions are.
- What the boundary conditions are.
- Which systems it applies to.
- How uncertainty is represented.
- How variables transform when coordinates change.
- What results would conflict with the law.

The equation provides relational form.

Measurement procedures connect symbols to phenomena.

Therefore:

> **A physical law is not an isolated equation, but a structure composed of equation, operation, conditions, transformations, and testable consequences.**

Without operational meaning, an equation moves only among symbols.

Without relational form, measurements struggle to form unified constraints across conditions.

---

## XII. Laws Gain Content by Exclusion

An expression capable of accommodating every result has no physical constraint.

The content of a law comes from excluding possibilities:

> Under specified conditions, some results should not occur.

Predicting `x = 8` also means that `x = 100` under the same conditions does not satisfy the relation.

Therefore:

> **A law does not contain every result; it places testable constraints on possible results.**

The clearer the constraint, the clearer the risk of failure carried by the law.

If every deviation can be absorbed by indefinitely adding explanations, the law gradually loses physical content.

A mature law must state prediction, uncertainty, conditions, and failure criteria together.

---

## XIII. Prediction Sends Relation into the Unknown

Prediction is not a law seeing the future in advance.

Its structure is:

1. Give known conditions.
2. Transform through a stable relation.
3. Derive a result not yet measured.
4. Let the new measurement occur.
5. Compare prediction and record.

Therefore:

> **Prediction extends a maintained relation to a place where collision has not yet occurred.**

Predictive success does not turn a law into absolute truth.

It means the relation remained after crossing one new condition.

Predictive failure does not automatically reject only the core equation, because an experiment also depends on instruments, initial conditions, boundaries, and auxiliary theory.

But a law must enter a public procedure capable of distinguishing success, uncertainty, and failure, or it cannot bear testing.

---

## XIV. Counterfactuals Make Laws More Than Historical Summaries

A law does more than organize records that have already occurred.

It also expresses:

> If conditions were different, how should the result differ?

For example:

- If observational position changes, how do coordinates transform?
- If external action changes, how does measurement respond?
- If clocks follow different paths, how do readings correspond?
- If an experiment is rotated, does the relation remain?

Such "if–then" statements form counterfactual constraints.

A counterfactual is not arbitrary imagination.

It must state which conditions change, which relations remain, whether the operation is defined, and how the result would be measured.

Therefore:

> **A counterfactual holds by changing some differences while maintaining some relations.**

The power of a law comes not only from summarizing what occurred, but from constraining what could occur.

---

## XV. Correlation, Intervention, and Causal Narrative

Two quantities remaining correlated does not automatically mean one causes the other.

Stable correlation may arise from:

- Common conditions.
- Measurement coupling.
- Selection procedures.
- Hidden variables.
- Two aspects of one structure.

Discussing causation further requires actively changing one condition while controlling other relevant relations, then testing whether the result changes as predicted.

Thus:

> Observation establishes empirical correlation; intervention tests how the relation responds to change.

But not every physical law must be narrated as "one thing pushing another."

Some laws express constraints, conservation, symmetry, or global structure.

"The law causes the phenomenon" is not the only available narrative and should not re-establish law as a hidden actor.

---

## XVI. Finite Data Cannot Logically Guarantee the Future

Every experiment occurs only finitely many times.

One million stable past results do not logically guarantee that the next must be the same.

Saying "the past has always been stable, so the future will be stable" already uses the induction that needs explanation.

Therefore:

> **There is no completely presuppositionless logical bridge from finite experience to universal law.**

Extending a law into the unknown depends on a constrained expectation: new conditions can be placed within a previously tested relational class.

This expectation is not final proof.

It continues to carry the possibility of failure through new predictions.

A law does not leap from finite experience into eternal truth. It remains provisionally stable across expanding differences.

---

## XVII. Stability Across Difference Is Stronger Than Repetition Count

Repeating nearly identical conditions many times shows stability only within a narrow range.

Stronger support comes when a relation crosses:

- Different places.
- Different times.
- Different instruments.
- Different object instances.
- Different observers.
- Different scales.
- Different coordinates and units.

If the relation remains through these differences, or changes according to one transformation rule, it is more than a local coincidence.

Thus:

> **Confidence in a law comes not only from how many times it repeats, but from how many kinds of difference it can cross.**

Symmetry and transformation rules do not eliminate the problem of induction.

They rewrite the vague expectation that "the future should resemble the past" into relations that can be tested one by one.

---

## XVIII. Theoretical Networks and Public Testing

An isolated relation may be only a local fit.

Physical laws usually belong to a mutually constraining network:

- Shared systems of units.
- Shared instrument calibration.
- Shared transformation rules.
- Different experiments measuring the same parameter.
- Predictions in one domain agreeing with another.
- Independent paths converging on the same result.

No isolated equation grounds all objectivity by itself.

As with public clocks, multiple experiments, standards, records, and theoretical relations jointly form a network of constraint.

Therefore:

> **The stability of a law comes not only from fitting its own data, but from remaining coherent with other measurement relations.**

Publicity does not mean everyone holding the same belief.

It means different people can reconstruct, compare, and challenge the same relation through open procedures.

---

## XIX. Deterministic, Statistical, and Approximate Laws

A law need not give one unique result every time.

It can give a probability distribution, correlation structure, or statistical range.

Different individual outcomes do not mean the law has failed; what matters is whether the distribution of many results continues to match the prediction.

Therefore:

> **Objectivity does not require an absence of randomness, but repeatably testable statistical structure within random outcomes.**

A law also need not be infinitely universal.

An approximate law that remains effective within a specified scale, speed, environment, and precision retains physical content.

The domain is not a note outside the law.

It is part of the law itself.

Failure outside that domain need not erase effective relations already established within it.

---

## XX. Are Laws Discovered or Created?

Variables, coordinates, units, equation forms, and synchronization methods contain construction.

But whether predictions continue to succeed is not decided by personal choice.

Laws therefore cannot be classified as wholly discovered or wholly created.

We can distinguish:

- Forms of expression are constructed through research.
- Equivalence among expressions is established through testing.
- The capacity to remain consistent with phenomena is constrained by reality.

Thus:

> **Laws as words and equations are created; the stable relations they capture are discovered through collision.**

People can create descriptions.

They cannot by will alone create continuing agreement between a description and future measurement.

"Discovery" and "creation" are not fully separate processes, but two sides of the same research activity.

---

## XXI. Do Laws Hold Without Observation?

We must distinguish a law's expression from the constraint toward which it points.

The expression includes variables, equations, units, domains, and measurement procedures.

Without observation, records, and language, no law exists as a stated law.

But this does not mean personal consciousness can make any prediction succeed or make any explanation fit later traces.

"What happened while no one observed" cannot be directly reported from a position outside all observation.

When observation occurs again, we face present traces: object states, instrument records, reports from other observers, and constraints among these relations.

Therefore:

> **Representationally, without observation, record, and theoretical activity there is no stated law. Physically, “the law continued while unobserved” is an extrapolation supported by present traces, cross-time tests, and predictions that may fail. Ontologically, that evidence does not authorize a direct report from outside all observation.**

"The law continued to hold while unobserved" is a stable extension supported by present traces, not a direct report brought back from outside observation.

---

## XXII. Inquiry Expresses Law; Results Constrain Expression

Seen from the side of inquiry:

- Researchers select variables.
- Recognizes repetition.
- Establishes equality.
- Writes equations.
- Maintain and revise the expression of laws.

Seen from the object-side:

- Measurements resist arbitrary interpretation.
- Predictions succeed or fail.
- Different observers are forced to calibrate.
- Some relations remain while others do not.

We therefore cannot say only that consciousness creates laws, nor can we establish laws as commanders outside consciousness.

More precisely:

> **Inquiry expresses stable relations as laws; new empirical results constrain which expressions can continue to hold.**

The form of law belongs to observation.

The constraint of law comes from collision.

The objectivity of law comes from that constraint not being arbitrarily rewritable.

---

## XXIII. The Circle of Lawful Measurement

Recognizing a law requires first deciding which experiments count as the same kind.

Deciding that experiments are the same kind requires knowing which differences do not affect the law.

Testing descriptive equivalence requires comparing measurement predictions.

Defining one physical situation requires knowing which predictions should be the same.

Extending the past into the future requires stable law.

Confirming stable law requires future measurement to occur.

These loops cannot be terminated by an absolute law standing outside every experiment.

Actual research stabilizes through the whole network: variables, instruments, units, transformations, predictions, and phenomena mutually constrain one another.

Therefore:

> **A physical law is not the first principle that independently grounds measurement; it is also a relation formed and revised as the measurement network repeatedly collides.**

Laws support predictions.

Predictions in turn test laws.

Theory organizes experiments.

Experiments in turn change theory.

---

## XXIV. Boundaries of the Study

This study has not demonstrated that:

- Every natural law is merely a linguistic habit.
- Mathematics can uniquely derive the true law from finite data.
- Symmetries necessarily hold exactly at every scale.
- Predictive success means a theory is ultimately true.
- The world stops or loses structure without observation.
- Laws can be arbitrarily created by personal consciousness.
- Physical objectivity requires observers, instruments, and protocols to disappear.
- Transformation relations have completely solved the problem of induction.

It proposes conceptual conditions under which laws become public physical knowledge:

- Variables are measurable.
- Experiments are comparable.
- Descriptions are transformable.
- Symmetries are testable.
- Equations have operational meaning.
- Predictions can fail.
- Uncertainty and domain can be stated.
- Different observers can reconstruct results.
- Stable relations can continue to be tested across difference.

The content of concrete laws must be determined by concrete theories, instruments, and experiments.

## XXVI. How Laws Become Possible

Now return to the question in the title.

Why can laws hold?

Not because a law stands as an independent existent behind phenomena and issues commands.

Not because finite experience logically guarantees an infinite future.

Nor because all observers, coordinates, and instruments give the same numbers.

Laws become possible because:

> **Measurement differences can be organized into comparable variables; experiments can repeat under equivalent conditions; descriptions and configurations can transform; some relations remain or change by rule; equations can generate predictions that risk failure; and new measurements continually constrain which relations can continue to be maintained.**

Yet saying "a law holds" can again establish law as an object waiting to be discovered.

Strictly speaking, there is not first a complete law and then phenomena obeying it.

This study does not install “occurrence” as another first entity. By the time a law can be stated, event-selection, comparison, transformation, compression, and prediction already determine one another within inquiry.

"Law" is the name given after this stable relation has been pointed out.

The following must therefore be taken back:

> A law does not stand outside occurrence and explain occurrence.

More precisely:

> **When change enters comparable measurement, stable transformation, and fallible prediction, occurrence is maintained in the form of law.**

This study does not stand outside law either.

It selected variables, established relations, compressed an argument, and predicted that these sentences could jointly point toward one structure.

It is itself one manifestation of the process by which law forms.

## Where This Stage Lands

One methodological note and five extension studies form a path that adds conditions layer by layer:

> **00 What It Means to Hold**: constitution, expression, physical constraint, and ontology.
> **01 How Objects Become Possible**: distinction, boundary, reidentification, and attribution of subject and world.
> **02 How Space Is Organized and Measured**: spatial relations entering equal length, units, and coordinates.
> **03 How Time Is Organized and Measured**: order and traces entering cycles, counting, and synchronization.
> **04 How Public Objectivity Becomes Possible**: local measurement entering calibration, closure, and independent testing.
> **05 How Laws Become Possible**: public relations entering equations, prediction, counterfactuals, and failure.

But this is not a completed route deriving physics from a foundation.

It examines how local objects, public measurement, and physical knowledge can still work after absolute subjects and objects are withdrawn. It is a compatibility study with the root texts, not a derivation of physics from a root sentence.

This stage ends here.

If the work continues, it should not add a higher absolute foundation.

It should take this research into concrete cases, mathematical models, and actual measurement, where it can meet real collision.
